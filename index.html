ABOUT THE PROJECT INTRODUCTION

about the project

“World Happiness: A Closer Look” was created in the 2020 Summer Session course, Digital Humanities 101, an introductory digital humanities class taught under the supervision of Dr. Ashley Sanders Garcia and TA Madison Faulis at the University of California, Los Angeles.

Students of the course were assigned a final project where they were tasked to choose a public dataset to analyze and conduct research based on an argument they formulated. With this research, students were also expected to create a website to portray their findings. The final project itself tests students to present their research in a narrative form, including visualizations created through the various tools that were taught throughout the course.

Our team Cloud 6 chose to analyze the World Happiness Report 2020 dataset which focused on collecting the happiness scores of countries and regions, as well as the factors that made up this score. To do this, we undertook the 3 levels of digital humanities:

sources

processing

presentation

  • SOURCES - To answer our research questions comparing happiness in the Nordic and Asian Tiger Economy countries and determining if happiness is an individual or national matter, we used the World Happiness Report datasets from the years 2011-2019 that was collected by the Gallup World Poll. The dataset includes a sample size of 2,000-3,000 people from each country that they survey. The amount of countries that are included in the World Happiness report fluctuate each year, but this past report included 154 countries. The dataset for the World Happiness Report broke down the happiness scores of each country by individual factor as well as the upper whisker and lower whisker, which are the variance of the reported scores by individuals in the prospective country. To learn more about our data sources, visit the Data page. 
  • PROCESSING - The dataset provided by the World Happiness Report is comprehensible and clean. There were no erroneous entries, which made it easy to read and understand the data. We used Open Refine and Google Sheets to do minimal cleaning on the data, as well as Tableau and Plotly to make our data visualizations. We limited our data to just the Nordic and Asian Tiger Economies and to the years of 2011 to 2019, though the original dataset included data for some countries beginning in the year 2006 but the first World Happiness Report was released in 2012 using data collected in 2011. To process the data, we condensed the dataset to the years and countries that our project focused on, specifically the Nordic region as well as the Tiger Economy countries.  
  • PRESENTATION - Our goal with our project was to find out further reasoning for the ranking of countries made by the World Happiness Report. Happiness seems hard to define by itself, and determining a whole country’s happiness seems a little arbitrary because of the difficulty of recording the happiness of a whole country based on individual surveys and outside knowledge like GDP and healthy life expectancy. We wanted to further explore the idea explained in Silencing the Past that declared that “Human beings participare in history both as actors and narrators” (Trouillot 2). The World Happiness Report seems to exemplify what it means to be both part of those written about and of those who wrote it, and we made sure to emphasize this in our methods of presentation. For our presentation, Mobirise, a web design application, was used for the initial structure of the website, and other tools and frameworks like Bootstrap, Visual Studio Code, and Adobe Photoshop were employed during the development of the website.
